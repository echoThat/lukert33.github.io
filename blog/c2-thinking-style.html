Applying Categories to My Mind


Gregorc Thinking Style

As you can see from the above graph, the Gregorc thinking style test categorizes me more in terms of what I'm not than what I am. My mind style is, to roughly equal extents, Concrete-Random, Abstract-Random, and Abstract-Sequential. I do not seem to have the attributes of a Concrete-Sequential thinker, at least per one questionairre.

What does it mean that I tend away from Concrete-Sequential thinking? I went to Dr. Gregorc's page about mind styles to learn more. I must say, I disagree with the notion that I am averse to or lack the following:

  • an affinity for getting facts
  • predictable situations
  • applying ideas in pragmatic ways

While it's true that I'd rather not have every day be predictable, I generally am in favor of predictability when it comes to doing work. I also like to think that I prefer to operate on the basis of facts, and that I'd rather apply ideas than simply come up with them, write them down, and put them in a drawer. To not test an idea out in some practical way is to not fully generate the idea. As Milton wrote in his Areopagitica, "Let her and Falsehood grapple; who ever knew Truth put to the worse in a free and open encounter?" An idea requires a free and open encounter to become anything resembling a truth.

That said, the two entries on Abstract-Random and Concrete-Random do resonate with my experience. I would agree, I suppose, that I fit Gregorc's definition of a Random-Sequential thinker. One should be aware of one's flaws, so I think the cautionary tales are the lessons to take from this. To that end, here are tendencies that sound familiar, and that I should take note of:

  • a dislike of formal reports
  • the struggle to keep detailed records
  • a preference to have options, as opposed to not
  • a distase for dictatorial/authoritarian personalities
  • aversion to the need to justify my feelings
  • aversion to work in a restrictive environment

Some of those distastes feel worthy of note. I should account for my dislike of formal reports, and find a solution for generating such reports. I should realize that I need to strive to keep detailed records when the situation calls for it. Do I need to develop a taste for authoritarian personalities and restrictive environments? I don't see the point.

Fixed versus Growth Mindset

Dr. Carol Dweck, along with Dr. Gregorc, is has done prominent research on learning styles. Unlike Gregorc, Dweck outlines a more absolute difference between two ways of being. Dweck describes Fixed Mindsets and Growth Mindsets, and the latter is obviously preferable to the former.

Essentially, a Growth-minded person acknowledges their own limitations and, having done so, seeks to expands them. A Fixed-minded person perceives their capacity as set and unchangeable. It seems clear that our society encourages Growth-mindsets explicitly, but implicitly it may be that our culture of test-based and career-based identity thumps people into Fixed-mindsets I would suggest that at least two of the major cultural forces in world history--Christian Calvinism and Caste-based Hinduism--certainly lay rich foundations for a society of fixed-mindsets.

Luckily, our technologically-oriented world favors the growth-minded. I agree with Carol Dweck that this is plainly the better way to be.

Practical Ways Forward

Having reflected on the work Gregorc and Dweck, I think that a system of more organization and discipline will allow me to work better in DBC and onward. Actually, I think these blog posts are the perfect blend of routine, formal reporting, and practical wrestling with ideas that I need. In writing fiction, I constantly struggle to tend to my current projects daily, but I know how useful that is. Blogging provides an opportunity for that level of reflection and processing in my coding life.
