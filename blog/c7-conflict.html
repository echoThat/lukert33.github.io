Modes of Conflict, Patterns of Shame

Nov 9, 2014

Here's an interview with myself on the subject of conflict, drawing on the compelling categories of shame presented by the Southdown Institute.


Discuss a time you had an emotional conflict with someone. What was it about? What happened? What was the source of the conflict?

A few years ago my then-housemate (still friend) and I got into a dispute about the portioning of rent at our apartment. There were decisions to be made; I'd moved from being a part time resident of the place to full time, and people occupying each bedroom were changing around. We had to figure out rents. At one point, I misunderstood something he said. I'd found this apartment for the whole crew--no minor dedication of time, as any New Yorker knows. This housemate and I had also worked hard together to get out of a prior lease commitment when it turned out the place had bed bugs, and the land lord was going to let us move in without telling us.

In the new place, during the discussion about rent splits, I took something my housemate said to mean that because I didn't yet live there full time, he and the others could decide to kick me out. I fumed over this for a day before confronting him on this. To his credit, he realized how angry I was and took the issue seriously. He assured me that I'd misunderstood him. Things resolved pretty quickly after that. But airing it out was as essential as disarming a bomb.

How did you handle that conflict?

I didn't stew very long on the issue, but I did let myself get quite angry about it because I felt that I was not being given credit for having found the apartment. I wish I could have kept a cooler head; it was only a discussion about money. That said, I am glad I didn't put off discussing my feelings for too long.

Did your actions make the conflict better or worse?

It was my misapprehension that started the conflict, so ultimately it was on me. But I don't think I made it worse once the seed was planted. It was too important for me to avoid.

If you could go back, what would you do differently, if anything?

I'd like to say that I should have clarified the issue at the moment I took offense. But really, that was in the course of a long discussion. Giving the issue its own time, a day or so later, was the only way to deal with it.

What did you learn from this experience?

I learned that friends are never going to have any problem with you airing out your grievances. People are inclined to help their friends, and communication is a balm that can fix a lot of problems.

Relative to the Compass

As far as the compass of shame goes, I tend toward Avoidance, the southern point. I am apt to exempt myself from a conflict by convincing myself that I'm above it. It's probably an arrogant pattern of behavior, though I will say it allows me to keep a level head even if something does blow up. I just have to be better about addressing conflicts that do need addressing, and not letting them go unresolved.
