Formulating Questions

November 16, 2014 by Luke

Jon Skeet's post on asking Stack Overflow questions was immensely useful. He emphasized the need to know about the interface that your answerer will be using. On Stack Overflow, this gives you cause to write a subject line as a question and to be aware that the first few lines of your question will appear as a preview.

When asking someone a question face to face, it's also necessary to pay attention to their experience of hearing you out. I have a tendency to overload people with information when looking for answers. Context is necessary, but it's just as necessary to make that context digestible. In live conversation, just as online, a long outflow of information will provoke a tl;dr.

Thinking about this in terms of the open source community, a group of experts with bankable skills that they elect to share, I realized what a privilege it is to have access to people who have the answers you need. Treat it like a privilege. Evaluate your question and take the time to frame it eloquently. Make life easy on the person you are asking time and effort of.

In my old job, we worked with a lawyer who we would go to if vital questions arose during a contract negotiation that we either didn't quite know how to handle, or for which we needed to know the legal basis. He's a pleasure to talk to, has a deep body of knowledge about entertainment law...but he charges by the hour! That latter fact was not something a small business like ours could afford to forget. We'd pass our questions back and forth in the office while typing them up to send to him. Preparation pays (or, in this case, saves).

Don Kahn made a great point about the value of bringing a prospective answer along with your questions. Even if the answer is empirically wrong, it provides the askee with a concise insight in your thought process, and an answer that they can react to while forming their own. Showing your own wrong answer makes giving you a correct answer that much easier.
